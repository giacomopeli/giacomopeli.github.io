Finally I bought a Thinkpad X230

My new Thinkpad X230 and a little reflection on mid-range and low-end laptops.
17 September 2022 · 11 min · Giacomo Peli

It all started when I needed a netbook that would allow me to focus on writing; a task that does not require high computing power, at least not from the computer. I started to do my research, but in the end I always found always netbooks that were too old, with Intel Atom processors, battered and way too expensive for their value. So I started looking for newer models with a Intel Celeron 4020 processor. In absolute terms these computers do not cost much, but it is difficult to to find one that is compact enough and has good portability.

I therefore decided to narrow down my search. The characteristics of the computer I was looking for were

  • Panel under 13 inches
  • Good keyboard

Something really interesting I found is the Asus E210, a small notebook under 12 inches that came out recently. I went to see it in a physical store and I have say that it is more than decently built. Online you can’t find anything under 170€, a price that definitely exceeded my budget.

Good keyboard? Compact? Well, Lenovo Thinkpad X series. Thus began my search for the Thinkpad X220 and X230. The X220 have one of the best keyboard ever built on laptops, made by IBM, but this model mount second-generation Intel processors, instead, the X230 mounts third-generation Intel processors, but have an updated keyboard compared to the X220. I have tried both keyboards and let’s face it, the keyboard on the X230, the one I am writing this article with, is a very good keyboard and, although many people prefer the old one made by IBM, in my opinion there is not that much difference in build quality. On the plus side, the IBM keyboard can be mounted on the X230 without any problem and by fiddling with the BIOS a bit. Replacing the keyboard would be a splurge among future upgrades, but I am getting along very well already with the upgraded version and do not want to increase the cost of this project. Upgrading the keyboard of the X230 with that of the X220 would provide the best of both worlds, so I chose the X230.

The ebay auction#

The first auction I attended was of an X230 with broken arrow keys, 128 Gb SSD, the battery capacity was not optimal and the overall condition was not among the best. I bid 105€ and did not win it for 106€. I was both disappointed at not getting it and glad for not getting a bad deal, and it really would have been.

The second auction I followed was of an X230 declared as good as new with no obvious signs of use, a new 256 Gb SSD, but the battery capacity was stated to be 10%. Intrigued by this, I jokingly put in as a maximum bid 100€ knowing that I would not win it, given the previous auction figure. Could I have purchased a better model at a lower price? No. Instead, that’s exactly what happened. Forgetting about the auction, I find a payment notification with amount of 92€ (excluding shipping). Jackpot!

Unboxing and good vibes#

The package arrives from Germany early. I feel like a child unwrapping his own birthday present and, in fact, it was. The Thinkpad was not in its original packaging of course, but what could I have expected from a 10 year old notebook? Nonetheless, after unpacking I felt a wonderful feeling, which I very rarely experience with technology.

Battery check#

According to the seller’s description, I had already considered that the first thing to be replaced would be the battery, but I decided to to check it anyway. It is a 9-cell model, the oversized version. After slipping it out of its housing I notice that it is the original one marked Lenovo. I then installed Linux Mint, putting off installing Arch until a few hours later. From the live feed of Linux Mint I notice that the battery is at 80% capacity. Basically, the vendor had stated that the battery had lost just more than 10% capacity and not that the capacity was 10% as I thought it was. This could not have worked out better for me. The 9-cell battery original Lenovo for Thinkpad X220 and X230 cost quite a bit, as third-party ones degrade much more easily and are unlikely to maintain constant capacity over time. I therefore install Linux Mint, check that all the hardware is working properly and uninstall it.

Arch Linux, btw#

I notice that the installation of Linux Mint is super smooth and I have to say that I was quite sad to have to format the disk to install Arch, but I did it anyway otherwise I could never have said I use Arch, btw.

Ah, I forgot to specify that the seller did not have any operating system installed so I assume that the SSD of 256 Gb that was mounted is new.

My custom setup for Thinkpad X230#

I don’t like to waste time fiddling with configuration files to make visually beautiful but impractical receptions. I have already explained what I think about it in a previous article. Basically I use KDE as it comes out of the installation and make some light modifications. On this Thinkpad, Wayland runs like a charm and even the 1:1 gestures work with the tiny trackpad on these models.

I installed Move Windows to Center a KWin’s script to move unmaximized windows to the center of the desktop and assigned it the shortcut Meta+C (I set the Windows key on each of my devices as the Meta key). You can find it here.

The ThinkVantage button, which on Windows would have a very specific function, on Linux turns out to be as useless as it is useful when we consider that its function can be customized as we wish. I set the shortcut to open the KDE overview, a function that I love about this de and find really useful.

This is, nothing more and nothing less. Quick to set up and effective. How much time would I have wasted to make a rice that consumes 200MB less of RAM? Remind me to upgrade the RAM to 16GB when I’ll saturate the 8GB on this Thinkpad. I’m ironic, of course.

Because I want to go as stock as possible and support KDE I am writing this article with Kate. the text editor from the KDE suite that can’t miss in any of my installations. I also installed markdownpart which allows me to write in markdown format having a real-time preview on the side. I usually use Mark Text a great software to write in markdown, but compiling on Arch is really time consuming and not worth it.

I modified my bashrc file by inserting pfetch and a script that tells me the processor temperature.

Analyze temperatures and fan to avoid changing thermal paste#

Processor temperatures are slightly higher than a low-power eighth-generation Intel U-series processor, but they are still around 50°C, and at this very moment when I am writing this it is 43°C and the fan has not started, as if it were fanless.

At full speed it even reaches over 70°C. The air outlet slots are located on the left side of the laptop and if you rest your left hand on the desk to the side of the Thinkpad you can feel the warm air, but nothing too annoying. It will be pleasant to use this winter!

Several online users have complained that the fans on these models are quite noisy. True, the fan can be heard, but in very light and only at high load. I have very fine hearing and usually the fans on desktop desktops bother me a lot, but the one of my Thinkpad most people would not even hear it.

Having made these considerations, I have decided not to disassemble the Thinkpad just to replace the thermal paste, but I will only take the opportunity when I make upgrades.

Upgrades I’m considering#

Among the various upgrades I have seen online there are:

Retrofitting the X220 keyboard#

As mentioned above, the keyboard on the X230 can be replaced with the better one on the X220. IBM keyboards in Italian are hard to find; it is a very popular upgrade and one that many X230 owners make. After writing this article I can say that I don’t have any need it, I am already very comfortable with it.

Here you can find a video of Classic Dome about the keyboard swap.

Express Card with 3x USB 3.0#

On online ecommerce stores you can find express cards compatible with three USB 3.0 ports. Having three additional USB 3.0 ports on this Thinkpad would not be bad, but it still remains a whim since I would not need them. Also, these expresscards remain always powered and drain power from the battery, which is definitely inconvenient.

RAM upgrade#

I’ve bought it with 8 Gb yet, upgrading it to 16 Gb would be useless.

Storage upgrade#

I bought this Thinkpad with a new 256GB SSD, and I don’t feel like I need to increase the space further. However, this Thinkpad has a WWAN module that allows internet connection via a sim card. I don’t know if I will ever use this feature and, in fact, I am considering replacing this WWAN card with a 128GB msata SSD where I could place my Linux /home folder.

Display IPS#

One of the downsides of this Thinkpad is that it mounts a TN panel, which is quite ugly and impractical for everyday use. Perhaps, for writing, I could compromise with this, although at the moment it remains a pain to see the colors of different shades between the top and bottom of the display. While looking for the right angle to make up for this gap between too light and too dark, I cannot find a reason to keep this panel. Then I remember that the prices for an IPS panel are around 60/70€ and the TN panel is starting to suit me.

A little reflection#

Every component can be replaced at this Thinkpad: RAM, SSD, WLAN, WWAN, bluetooth, display, keyboard, expresscard, and who knows what else. This gives me pause; in today’s notebooks it is a plus when RAM is not soldered in and a luxury when the slots on the motherboard are two.

This Thinkpad model, but other references in general, have held their own against other notebooks for a good 10 years and, in my opinion, it is far ahead of many low and mid-range notebooks on the market today and coming out in the next few years. Thinkpads hardly get old.

In benchmarks the processor in this X230, an i5 3320M, performs better than an i3 6100U. The figure is realistic and I can comfirm it as I have had a chance to try both processors. The fluidity of this X230 is absurd considering that it is 10 years old, there is no lag and no slowdown.

The portability is great and on par with a thin notebook, maybe not a Thinkpad X1 Nano.

What computer could an average user buy today in the 100€-200€ range with these features? None. There is little innovation in the technology market, and few things can be found that are actually worth what they cost, justifying their purchase. It may be because of rising production and energy costs, the innovation curve or lack of teams of visionaries, but what I wrote is a fact.

The used market is hardly considered by average users, perhaps due to lack of product information or perhaps out of fear of receiving a product that does not conform to the description. Finding a good product in the used market is difficult, it requires knowledge, a good amount of free time, and the ability to get by on making a repair or a small upgrade. At a time like the present, it is critical that the average user’s approach to used equipment change.

It is not about giving new life to a now-discontinued product, but using it until it has a useful life, and beyond.

Often we replace our tools prematurely, getting caught up in new models and overly appealing advertisements. The euphoria after purchase lasts a few hours, but we realize that, after all, the new product is not at all different from what we had before. It is also the fault of companies that fail to design products that bond the user with the product they buy.

In this regard, I recommend that you watch episode 9x19 of The Big bang Theory.

This Thinkpad succeeds very well in this being amazing and I am not surprised that quite a few have been sold, sure at the release the price was high, but people are still buying them now and the reason is obvious: it is a well-made product. The only thing I’m still pondering is if I had purchased a Thinkpad X230 in 2013 would I still be using it? Are those who bought it in 2013 still using it?
